If you‚Äôve been on Linux for more than a month, you know the feeling. You install Arch because you want the latest packages. Then, something breaks, and you miss the stability of Debian. You try Fedora for a “middle ground,” but then you miss the AUR.

I have spent years in this loop. I thought I was looking for a better desktop environment or a faster package manager. But I was actually looking for NixOS.

As one YouTuber perfectly put it: “If Debian and Arch had a baby, it would be NixOS.”

1. The Best of Both Worlds

The “Baby” analogy works because NixOS takes the two things we love most about Linux and merges them:

  • The Stability of Debian: In NixOS, your system is “immutable.” If you make a mistake, you don’t have to spend three hours in a TTY trying to fix it. You just reboot and select the previous “Generation” from the boot menu. It‚Äôs a literal time machine.
  • The Freshness of Arch: Nixpkgs is one of the largest and most up-to-date repositories in existence. You get the bleeding-edge software you crave, but without the “bleeding” part.

2. Infrastructure as Code (The “Aha!” Moment)

In every other distro, your system is a mystery. You install a package here, tweak a config file in /etc there, and six months later, you have no idea why your WiFi works or why your themes look the way they do. If your SSD dies, that setup is gone forever.

In NixOS, your entire computer is a single text file.

Everything‚Äîfrom your wallpaper and your gaming drivers to your custom Hyprland keybinds‚Äîis declared in a .nix file.

The Kid Version: Imagine if you had a magic Lego manual. Instead of building the castle yourself and hoping it doesn’t fall over, you just hand the manual to a robot. The robot builds the exact same castle every single time. If you want a tower to be blue instead of red, you just change one word in the manual and the robot fixes it.

3. Mixing Stable and Unstable (Zero Stress)

This was the “killer feature” for me. In traditional distros, you are usually locked into one “branch.” If you want a newer version of one specific app on a stable distro, you have to deal with “dependency hell.”

NixOS doesn’t care. Because of how Nix stores files (in the /nix/store with unique hashes), I can run my entire core system on the Stable channel for rock-solid reliability, but pull my Games or Video Editors from the Unstable channel to get the latest features. They never fight each other. They live in total harmony.

4. The “Fixed Once, Fixed Forever” Rule

On Arch, I used to keep a notebook of “fixes” for when my audio acted up or my second monitor didn’t wake up. Every time I reinstalled, I had to find that notebook.

On NixOS, once I solve a problem in my configuration.nix, it is solved forever.

  • Buying a new laptop? Copy the file.
  • Setting up a home server? Copy the file.
  • Breaking the system at 2 AM? Just roll back.
